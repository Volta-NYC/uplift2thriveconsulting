Linda Storm’s work is a deeply personal fusion of Mythical Realism, storytelling, and music — rooted in childhood wonder, global artistic experiences, and a lifelong connection to nature and imagination. Through The StormHold, she is transforming a historic adobe space into a multi-sensory cultural sanctuary where art, sound, and human connection converge. Guided by legacy, intuition, and collaboration with her husband Gary, Linda envisions a space that honors ancient traditions while fostering modern creative exchange — inviting visitors into an intimate, immersive experience that sparks inspiration, community, and a renewed sense of wonder.

**Linda, your work blends Mythical Realism with storytelling and music. What first drew you to this intersection of art forms?** I lived my youth on a few acres in the tiny historic village of Alden, NY. Outside, I marveled at dew drops on spider webs, and trees that looked like they were cloaked around human forms. I held ancient fossils that whispered stories of a different time in the place where I was standing. I remember feeling the sky become part of me as I breathed, and I found forever in the black between the stars.

Inside, I had a record player in my play room. Music filled me with feelings beyond the five senses and I danced till my feet were sore. During quiet times I read from a set of children’s books that hugged myths, fairytales, and legends between their elaborate covers. Ancient Goddess stories, with their wisdom, patience, and magical abilities to birth hope and inspiration enchanted me. I drew them into reality whenever I had paper, which was usually when my mother opened a package of nylons.

My vocabulary was not full enough to describe the abundance of my life experiences. Drawing and painting became the languages for me to express myself.

Storytelling through art and music are the most ancient modes of human communication. I’ve used them in my experience as a mother, Montessori teacher, and in my world travels as an artist. I painted a mural outside a school in Costa Rica while my husband Gary played his guitar. Children gathered in delight as flocks of Scarlett Macaws flew over our heads screeching like dinosaurs. In Italy, I created a temporary outdoor installation titled A New Vision for the Same Place, that people entered into through a paper curtain while live music was performed. Another time in Italy, Gary led a spontaneous sing-a-long during a different one of my exhibits. At Chateau Orquevaux Art Residency in France, I wrote poetry and shared them as titles to my paintings while Gary played live music. In Santa Fe, NM, I, shared my art during small concerts in my studio. The arts  allow us to raise each other up to being the beautiful humans we are meant to be.

**StormHold sounds like more than just a gallery, it’s an experience. What vision are you hoping people feel and take away when they step into the space?** Long before I existed there were stories painted on cave walls. Very near where we  now live there are Native American petroglyphs, and ruins where a bone flute was found. The StormHold is a legacy to those ancient communities, and a haven where cultural experiences involve all of our senses, and where ideas are born, cultivated, and shared.

Santa Fe is a hub for tourism and so my mission is to create a worldwide family through the arts in the intimate setting of a house made of mud.

**Renovating a 100-year-old adobe home is a big undertaking. How has that process influenced your creativity and perspective as an artist?** I had a dream, literally, about The StormHold. It was during COVID. Gary and I imagined it and hunted for a place for a few years and finally found one. Our architect friend told us it had good bones. We bought it. I sat there alone many times and felt a lot of love. Then, when I tried to update it, I felt like my bite was more than I could chew. The place had been neglected for too long. It needed so much work, and with talks of tariffs and prices rising for construction my dream fizzled. Well, not really. It hid inside me until I could not hold it back.

Then we decided to sell the property. I secretly wished I could keep it but thought we couldn’t afford it. So many people wanted to buy it! They offered such ridiculously low prices that we refused them all. When the realtors wanted to renew the contract, we chose instead to keep the property.

My dad renovated houses as a hobby. He was an artist and beautified everything he could. When I was a tween he bought an old house in Buffalo, NY. We lived in it while he remodeled and then he sold it and bought another to improve. I loved helping him and seeing the final results. Finally, he built his dream home, a geodesic dome. I also helped with that.

He loved the adobe architecture in New Mexico. I think of him as I work on The StormHold and wish he could see the place, and maybe he can, even though I can’t see him. I feel him with me, that’s for sure. Gary, not a handy guy, is happy to work on his radio show or play his guitar while I renovate with our trusty contractor. The StormHold was just approved for a grant from the City of Santa Fe for an outdoor security system.

To succeed financially, we plan for various revenue streams. The Guy in the Groove, a superb vintage vinyl record store, rents space in The StormHold. He doesn’t sell online. The gallery rooms will be rented for small weddings, and other gatherings. We will host dinners with ancient recipes that honor specific cultures with storytelling bards. There will be sound healings, record listening sessions, adult and children’s story times, Paint with a Partner Classes taught by me, and whatever other creative opportunities fit our mission. We’ve already been approached by musicians requesting a stopover during their tours to play an intimate set for a small group. Our biggest and most important need right now are funds for central heating and air conditioning.

Once it is complete, I know more people will want to be there than can fit, so we will host some events for more than one night. The whole process requires creative thinking, and there is great satisfaction in collaborating on a job well done. It is a huge endeavor that can overwhelm me at times, but being an artist with goddess energy, and my experiences with my dad, and Gary’s never-ending support in all possible ways, and my friends telling me that The StormHold is going to Kick Ass, and you reaching out for me to share my story here **–** cheer me on!

**With your husband Gary involved through music and radio, how do you see sound and visual art working together to create a deeper connection for visitors?** Gary’s radio show Oil of Dog has been broadcasting for decades. We were even married on the radio! People of all ages listen to his show worldwide. Previous to COVID, musicians who were fans of Gary’s radio show discovered my social posts about my art studio and asked if they could perform there while on tour. I just kept responding to creativity with a YES! We even had punk rock flamenco show. Our events became so popular we could not fit everyone in. Some very famous musicians performed for our small audiences and everyone mingled afterward. We discovered that when people are surrounded by art and live music, sparks of energy lead to meaningful conversations, creativity expands, and new connections are made expounding the joie de vivre. We need all of that now more than ever. That is why we call the StormHold a haven.

**As StormHold grows into a cultural hub, what role do you hope it plays in connecting local communities with a wider, global artistic audience?** With The StormHold, we honor not only the ancient indigenous communities who gathered in painted caves and listened to bone flutes, we also honor the builder of the property that houses The StormHold. His grandson lives next door and fondly remembers his grandfather making the adobe bricks and spending holidays there as a child.  Energy emanates from the walls he built from those bricks and the hand hewn vigas in the ceiling carved from tree trunks. It was the first house in the area located on a prominent corner in Santa Fe, where one street is named after him, and the other street, Agua Fria, developed from the Camino Real, an ancient trade route, which later became Route 66.

It’s no surprise that The Stormhold is already attracting creative people. It is holding space for a diverse worldwide family linked by the arts. It will be a destination hub in Santa Fe, NM for intimate events, and for in-person shopping for art by me and other local artists, as well as gifts, art, vintage records, and jewelry. The events we host will be inclusive of all genders, all cultures, all colors, all ages including some age-appropriate entertainment for children.
